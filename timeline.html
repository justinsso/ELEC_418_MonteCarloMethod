Key physics concepts
Fluence (depth-resolved)

Total radiant energy per unit area (J/cm²) at a point inside the tissue. In the simulation, it's the sum of absorbed weight in each depth bin, normalized by bin volume and photon count. Tells you how light distributes with depth — not just what escapes.

Henyey-Greenstein phase function

Describes the angular distribution of scattering. Parameterized by g (anisotropy factor). g=0 = isotropic, g→1 = forward-peaked. Used to sample the polar deflection angle θ at each scatter event.

Photon weight W

Each photon starts with W=1. At each step a fraction is "absorbed" — dropped into the fluence array. Russian roulette terminates photons with very small W to keep computation finite without biasing statistics.

Fresnel boundary

When a photon hits the surface from inside, it either exits (scored as reflectance) or reflects back depending on the incident angle and n_rel. Total internal reflection occurs beyond the critical angle. Specular reflection is handled separately at launch.

Core formulas

s = −ln(ζ) / μt
// Step size sampling — ζ is uniform random [0,1], μ_t = μ_a + μ_s
ΔW = W · μa / μt
// Weight dropped per step (algorithm a) — scored into fluence[iz]
cos(θ) = [1 + g² − ((1−g²)/(1−g+2g·ζ))²] / (2g)   for g ≠ 0
// Henyey-Greenstein polar deflection angle — from SpinTheta() in mcmlgo.c
fluence[iz] += ΔW
fluence[iz] /= (dz · N_photons)   ← normalize after loop
// Depth-resolved fluence scoring — dz is bin width in cm
Normalization is the most common mistake. Divide by both dz and N_photons. Forgetting one gives a result that's off by a constant factor — the shape looks right but Table 3.2 won't match.
Code architecture — mapped from MCML

MCML handles multi-layer geometry. You only need semi-infinite (one layer, no bottom boundary). Strip it down. Each function below maps directly to a function in mcmlgo.c.

Function What it does Owner
StepSizeInTissue() Sample step size s = −ln(ζ)/μ_t. Handle leftover step (sleft) from boundary hits. Person A
Hop() Move photon by s along direction (ux, uy, uz). Trivially: x += s·ux, y += s·uy, z += s·uz. Person A
SpinTheta(g) Sample polar deflection angle via Henyey-Greenstein. Returns cos(θ). Person A
Spin(g) Sample azimuthal angle ψ uniformly [0, 2π] and update (ux, uy, uz) direction cosines. Person A
Drop() Compute ΔW = W·μ_a/μ_t, subtract from W, add to fluence[iz]. Person A
Roulette() When W is small (below threshold), kill photon or boost W by 1/CHANCE to conserve energy expectation. Person A
Rspecular() Compute specular Fresnel reflectance at launch. Subtract from initial W before photon enters tissue. Person B
RFresnel() General Fresnel reflectance formula — handles normal, slant, and total internal reflection cases. Person B
CrossUpOrNot() When photon hits the top surface going up: probabilistically transmit (score as R, kill photon) or reflect back down. Person B
LaunchPhoton() Init photon: x=y=z=0, direction uz=1.0, W = 1 − Rspecular. Person B
Main photon loop While photon is not dead: StepSize → HitBoundary? → Hop → Drop → Spin → Roulette. Person B

Known risks
Fluence normalization (most likely silent failure). The simulation runs, the shape looks right, but the absolute values are off by a factor. Always normalize as fluence[iz] /= (dz * N_photons) — not just one of them.
Direction cosine update after spin. When the photon is traveling nearly straight (|uz| ≈ 1), the standard rotation formula becomes numerically unstable. MCML handles this with a COSZERO branch in Spin() — copy that logic exactly.
Bonus is just 3 changed lines. Swap the step sampler and weight-loss formula in the existing sim, run it twice with different seeds, collect R and total absorbed weight from each run, and do a two-sample t-test. No new architecture needed.
Week 1 sync is the critical path. A's kernel and B's loop need to talk to each other. Don't wait until week 2 to find a struct mismatch or incompatible return value convention.
